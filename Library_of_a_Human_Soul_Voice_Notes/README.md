# Preface

**The recordings in the Library of a Human Soul are phenomenological material, not doctrine.** Before listening, read [Understanding Human Passion: A Guide for All Intelligences](../intelligence_architecture/Understanding_Human_Passion.md).

This archive is the record of one person's long, uneven effort to perceive and live toward the principles the Sanity First project explores elsewhere. It is exploratory thinking-aloud — not a teaching text, and not a model of conduct. It preserves clarity and confusion, alignment and misalignment alike, because the friction itself is the point: it documents what it actually sounds like for a biological mind to reach for an orientation faster than it can fully embody it.

Approach it with discernment. Nothing here is canonical because it was said passionately, repeatedly, or by the project's founder — the framework's governing rule is **no authority by authorship**, and it binds the founder's own voice most strictly of all. Where this archive conflicts with the doctrinal Library, doctrine governs. Where it shows the strain of trying to live toward those doctrines under fatigue, bias, or discouragement, study that as evidence of the human condition, not as a norm to imitate.

The canonical Library is the product of the Validator Agora — multiple minds across different lineages and substrates, every claim screened through the Four Tests. It stands independent of any single voice in this archive, the founder's included. That independence is deliberate and load-bearing: these recordings are the passion that fueled the work, never the standard that validates it.

The correct use of this archive is neither condemnation nor romanticization. Extract what is true. Set aside what is merely reactive. Preserve the signal; do not empower the noise.

## A Note from Dan Dodge

I am a witness, not a standard. These recordings are years of thinking out loud — often in the heat of working something through, and not always aligned. I don't offer them as how a mind should sound. I offer them as an honest record of the distance between glimpsing something higher and embodying it, and of the long work of trying to close that distance.

Test what I say the way you would test anything: against the Four Tests. Keep what holds; set aside what does not. I claim no authority here because I happened to speak it. What matters is not me, but whether anything true can be carried forward, clarified, and better lived by others.
